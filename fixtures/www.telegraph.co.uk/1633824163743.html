'I was a housekeeper for a wealthy family... and I was shocked by what I learnt about the super-elite'

The domestic workers of the super rich often know more about their clients than even their families know

By Luke Mintz 7 October 2021 • 5:00am

It was a particularly humid morning in the summer of 2010 when Stephanie Land first met the woman she would later nickname the ‘Cigarette Lady’. 

Once an ambitious writer who dreamt of university, Land had fallen on hard times after becoming a single mother in her 20s. She took a job as a housekeeper in Washington state to make ends meet. It meant hours of back-breaking labour, scrubbing the kitchens and bathrooms of America’s upper-middle class. 

She barely spoke to any of her clients; some never bothered to learn her name. Still, she got to know them intimately. She cleaned their medicine cabinets and dusted their photographs. She discovered the tragedies of their pasts, their financial difficulties, their marital woes.

To break up the monotony, she imagined elaborate narratives around each of them. ‘It was a way to entertain myself in a very lonely, boring job,’ Land, 43, tells me, speaking over the phone from her home in Montana. ‘Of course you start to wonder about the people who live there, I think that’s human nature. You start to know your clients in a way their families probably don’t.’

In her head, she gave each of her clients their own moniker: the ‘Sad House’, the ‘Farm House’, the ‘Chef’s House’. But none fascinated her more than the Cigarette Lady, a middle-aged housewife who lived next to a golf course with her construction magnate husband. Everything about the woman’s life glimmered with perfection. Her hair and make-up were ‘impeccable, flawless’. 

Downstairs was a framed photograph of her beside Tiger Woods; she was wearing a white shirt with matching shorts, the uniform of the American country club elite with whom Land had grown so familiar. The furniture in the house was sleek, modern and usually spotless – so much so that Land wondered why they paid her to clean. In the woman’s wardrobe was a tan cashmere cardigan. A few times, Land couldn’t resist trying it on; she imagined living there, inhabiting the woman’s life.

But the woman also harboured a secret. Land began to spot traces of cigarette ash around the house, and once found an ashtray behind the kitchen sink. It had been scrubbed clean with some vigour. Curiously, Land never saw any cigarettes – until one day, when she was exiting the house through the garage and noticed a freezer. She opened it. Hidden away at the back was a stack of Virginia Slims, an upmarket cigarette brand. Land allowed herself a smile of satisfaction.

‘I could picture her, with her chin resting in her hand, stamping out a cigarette… then getting up, tossing her hair a little, and emptying out the ashtray in the garage before carefully wiping it clean,’ Land recalls in her memoir, Maid: Hard Work, Low Pay, and a Mother’s Will To Survive. ‘It wasn’t about the smoking. It was the secrecy that fascinated me, the amount of energy she put into appearing perfect and clean.’

Published in 2019, Land’s bestselling book exposed the reality of life as an $8.55-an-hour (£6.34) housekeeper. And it left many middle-class readers wondering just how much their cleaner knows about them. She received messages from people who employed cleaners, amazed – and perhaps a little unnerved – by insights she’d gleaned into clients’ lives.

After interest from 10 production companies, the memoir was picked up for a Netflix series, Maid, released earlier this month; a fictionalised account of Land’s life for six years, over the late 2000s and early 2010s. American actor Margaret Qualley (Once Upon a Time in… Hollywood) plays the character based on Land. Qualley’s real-life mother, Andie MacDowell (Four Weddings and a Funeral, Groundhog Day), plays her on-screen mother.

More than anything, Land hopes the series illuminates the lives of domestic workers – a group people rely on for the smooth running of their day-to-day lives, but often know very little about. And she hopes its reach extends to the UK, where we employ close to one million cleaners. ‘I learned just how invisible that job is,’ she says. ‘It’s mainly unnoticed.’

Unlike most cleaners, Land grew up middle-class, in a two-child family in an Alaskan mountain town. She worked in a café in her early 20s but dreamt of writing books. She recalls thumbing The New York Times Book Review on her way to shifts, and collected brochures for a creative-writing programme at the University of Montana. A series of unforeseen events took her down a different path.

When she became pregnant unexpectedly, Land’s relationship with her boyfriend ruptured. He wanted her to have an abortion (she refused) and shouted at her regularly, once punching a hole through a door. She left him, taking her baby with her. For a while she was homeless. Eventually, she found housekeeping work in Washington. At her interview, she handed a CV to the manager of the cleaning company, who looked at it with confusion.

Each morning, Land dropped her child at nursery, then drove her rickety Subaru wagon to clean homes on Camano Island, a wealthy enclave near Seattle. It was painful work, not least because of her scoliosis, a condition in which the spine twists to the side.

But she soon became fascinated by the interior lives of the people who employed her. There was the ‘Porn House’, for instance. Adorning the walls were photographs of a married couple, smiling side by side in matching jumpers. A while in, she noticed they appeared to have started sleeping in separate bedrooms. 

On the man’s bedside table she found a bottle of lubricant; nearby, an open drawer revealed a stack of Hustler magazines. ‘Though I’d never fault anyone for looking at porn mags, I would fault them for leaving it out in the open for the cleaning girl to see,’ Land writes in her book. 

In the woman’s bedroom Land saw a stack of low-budget romance novels, with illustrations of topless, sculpted men embracing long-haired women. ‘I imagined them sleeping in different rooms… each fantasising about a different partner, and possibly a different life.’

Then there was the ‘Sad House’. In it lived a sickly man in his 70s, who seemed to have lost both his wife and son. He stored their ashes in two urns in his bathroom, and kept trinkets from his wife on his windowsill. On his noticeboard were to-do lists written in a woman’s handwriting – presumably hers. ‘He’d done everything right – good job, gorgeous house, married a woman he loved and travelled with. 

But despite all this he was still dying alone. [The job] had just been something to pay my bills, but now it felt like the work had an unexpected imprint on my life… I wondered what my clients did in the evenings. Where they sat, what they ate. My life had become so quiet. These people gave me something to look forward to, people to hope for.’

The ‘Clown House’ was decorated with creepy portraits of sad clowns, whose eyes seemed to stalk Land; at the ‘Hoarder House’, she had to sort through a bizarre mountain of clothes, dishes, backpacks and books abandoned on the floor. The owner described the pile guiltily as ‘my little secret’.

Some clients were cruel. One family, who lived in a ‘huge house’ atop a steep driveway, reprimanded her after some of her car’s oil leaked on to their tarmac. After that, they made her park far away and lug her heavy cleaning supplies up the hill. Others clearly distrusted her, refusing to leave her alone in the house in case she stole. One client would leave expensive jewellery out in her bedroom; Land couldn’t help feeling it was ‘bait’.

But others showed kindness. She talks fondly about one man, Henry, who lived with a pair of Australian Shepherds. One Friday, Henry was planning to cook lobster. As a thank you, he told her she could take a couple home. 

But even pleasant interactions like these had an undercurrent of awkwardness. It was difficult to escape the sense of injustice, she says; the feeling that she and her client had ended up in different layers of society through little fault of her own. In Henry’s house, she recalls spotting a bottle of Champagne next to a hot tub. ‘My body ached for… just one opportunity to drink Champagne in a hot tub.’ It was particularly difficult when her clients were the same age as her, or younger.

Land had other ambitions, too. She read voraciously, and began to document her life in an online blog. Eventually, with the help of a grant, she was able to start a bachelor’s degree in English at the University of Montana. Today, she lives in Montana with her husband, Tim, and three children. 

Writing allowed her to leave cleaning behind, though she still campaigns for domestic workers. During the early months of the pandemic she urged middle-class families to continue paying domestic help even when they were barred from attending to their duties.

Compared to her years as a cleaner, Land’s finances are transformed – so much so that she can now afford to hire her own cleaner. It’s something she never imagined she would do until a couple of years ago, when she injured her back and needed help cleaning her new home. 

She fiddled with her ring in awkwardness as she explained the job to the cleaner, a single mother called Michelle. Her voice cracked at one point as she asked her to clean a toilet. Once her back improved she avoided hiring another cleaner, but soon her husband will undergo major surgery, and may need help around the house.

The decision is weighing on her heavily. Many would argue that cleaning is a job like any other, one in which many people take pride. Some might see Land’s squeamishness as irrational; coming, perhaps, from a sense of guilt that she is now richer than the women she used to work with. And as a thoughtful, intelligent person, Land surely understands all that. But after the discomfort of last time, she says, ‘I can’t bring myself to do it.’

Maid is on Netflix now
